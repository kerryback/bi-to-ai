7  Agent Workflows

Module 5 showed agents automating a reporting pipeline — a linear sequence from query to finished deck. But agents can orchestrate far more than reports. This module explores workflows where the agent coordinates across multiple systems — email, calendars, and scheduling — turning unstructured inputs into organized actions.

7.1 Beyond reporting

The reporting pipeline in Module 5 follows a predictable path: query data, make a chart, write a summary, assemble a document. The steps are well-defined and the output is a file.

Many real-world workflows are messier. A manager wants to schedule a team meeting but availability is scattered across email threads. A sales rep needs to draft follow-up emails based on notes from a dozen calls. An executive assistant needs to reorganize next week’s calendar after a last-minute conflict.

These tasks are multi-step, repetitive, and involve reasoning over unstructured text — exactly what agents do well.

7.2 Email agents

An email agent connects to your email account and performs tasks that would otherwise require you to read, sort, and respond to messages manually.

Search and summarize. Instead of scrolling through your inbox, you ask the agent: “What emails came in about the Q2 budget this week?” The agent searches your email, retrieves the relevant messages, and returns a summary with key points from each.

Draft responses. You tell the agent: “Draft a reply to Sarah’s email accepting the meeting time but asking to move it to the conference room.” The agent reads the original message, drafts an appropriate reply, and presents it for your review before sending.

Organize and triage. The agent scans incoming messages and groups them: urgent items needing a response today, FYI messages that can wait, and newsletters to archive. Instead of processing 50 emails one by one, you review three categories.

The pattern is the same as the reporting pipeline: the agent chains tools together (search email, read message, generate text, send reply) in a loop. The difference is that the data source is your inbox instead of a database.

7.3 Calendar agents

A calendar agent reads and writes events on your calendar. It can answer questions (“When is my next meeting with the marketing team?”), find open slots (“What times am I free on Thursday afternoon?”), and create or move events.

The real power emerges when the calendar agent works with other tools. Combined with email, it can read a message proposing a meeting time, check your calendar for conflicts, and draft a reply confirming or suggesting alternatives — all from a single instruction.

Calendar agents also illustrate a key agent design principle: human-in-the-loop. Reading your calendar is low-risk; the agent should do it freely. Creating or moving events changes your schedule and affects other people. A well-designed calendar agent proposes changes and waits for your approval before executing them.

7.4 Using AI for scheduling

Scheduling is a constraint satisfaction problem. You have a set of people, each with their own availability, and you need to find a time that works for everyone. Traditionally, this means sending a poll, chasing responses, and manually comparing calendars.

An agent can do this conversationally. Here is the workflow:

  1. Gather constraints. The agent reads email threads where participants mentioned their availability: “I’m free Tuesday afternoon,” “Wednesday works except before 10,” “Any day but Friday.” These constraints are scattered across unstructured text — exactly the kind of input that LLMs excel at parsing.

  2. Check calendars. The agent looks at each participant’s calendar to find existing commitments. It combines the stated preferences from email with the hard conflicts from calendars.

  3. Propose options. The agent reasons through the constraints and proposes two or three time slots that work for everyone (or the largest subset, if no time works for all).

  4. Confirm and book. After the organizer approves, the agent sends calendar invitations and a confirmation email.

This is fundamentally different from a scheduling poll. A poll collects structured yes/no responses. An agent reads natural language, infers availability from context, and handles ambiguity (“I’d prefer mornings” is a soft constraint, not a hard one). The agent is doing what a skilled executive assistant does — reasoning through messy, human communication to find a workable solution.

7.5 Document processing agents

The scheduling example coordinates across calendars and email. Another powerful pattern coordinates across file formats. In many business workflows, the information you need is scattered across Excel spreadsheets, PDF reports, Word memos, and CSV exports — each in a different structure with different conventions.

A document processing agent ingests all of these, extracts the relevant information from each, and combines the results into a single analysis. The pattern looks like this:

Here are three examples of this pattern in finance and business:

M&A due diligence. An acquisition target sends a data room with a customer list (Excel), contract terms (PDF), the acquirer’s screening criteria (Word), and industry benchmarks (CSV). You tell the agent: “Evaluate this acquisition target.” It reads each file, extracts contract terms and change-of-control provisions from the PDF, applies the screening criteria to each customer, benchmarks against industry standards, and produces a due diligence memo suitable for the investment committee.

Loan portfolio risk analysis. A bank’s risk team has a loan tape with 220 loans (Excel), state banking regulations (PDF), and an internal risk policy memo (Word). The agent extracts regulatory thresholds from the PDF, pulls classification criteria from the Word document, assigns each loan a risk tier, flags regulatory violations, and produces a risk report with tier distribution and estimated reserves.

Portfolio consolidation. An investor holds positions across three brokerages, each reporting in a different format (two Excel workbooks and a CSV). The agent reads all three, standardizes the columns, resolves ticker symbol inconsistencies, sums positions that appear in multiple accounts, and produces a single consolidated view with total portfolio value and asset allocation.

The key insight is that these tasks are not difficult for a human — they are tedious. Reading a 30-page PDF to find three regulatory thresholds, then cross-referencing them against 220 rows in a spreadsheet, is exactly the kind of work that agents eliminate. The agent reads every format natively, never loses track of which rule came from which document, and finishes in minutes rather than hours.

7.6 Variance analysis agents

Variance analysis — comparing budgeted figures to actual results and decomposing the differences — is the core analytical task in FP&A. Every public company does it quarterly, and boards and investors ask “why did we miss?” after every earnings period.

An agent can perform a full variance decomposition from a single prompt. Consider this scenario:

Q1 actuals just closed. Operating income missed budget by $300K.

  • Revenue: Budget 100K units at $50 = $5M; Actual 95K units at $51 = $4.845M — $155K miss
  • COGS: Budget $30/unit; Actual $32/unit — $40K miss
  • SG&A: Budget $1.45M; Actual $1.555M — $105K miss

You prompt the agent: “Here is our Q1 budget vs. actuals spreadsheet. Decompose the $300K operating income miss into volume, price, cost, and discretionary spending drivers. Produce a waterfall chart and a summary memo for the CFO.”

The agent plans the decomposition, separates revenue variance into volume and price effects, breaks COGS variance into volume-driven and rate-driven components, itemizes SG&A overruns, generates a waterfall chart showing how each driver contributed to the $300K gap, and writes a CFO-ready memo — all from that single instruction.

This replaces two to four hours of manual FP&A work. The agent applies the same variance decomposition formulas an analyst would, but it does so instantly and presents the results in a format ready for executive review.

7.7 The MCP connection

These workflows become practical through MCP (Model Context Protocol), which you met in Module 4. Just as an MCP connector gave your agent access to a database, connectors exist for Gmail, Google Calendar, Outlook, Slack, and dozens of other services.

The pattern is consistent: install a connector, grant the agent access, and it gains new tools. A Gmail connector gives the agent search_email, read_message, and send_email tools. A Google Calendar connector gives it list_events, create_event, and find_free_time tools. The agent chains these tools the same way it chained SQL queries and chart generation in Module 5.

7.8 Designing safe agent workflows

As agents gain access to more systems, safety becomes more important. The principles from Module 6 apply here, plus a few specific to multi-system workflows.

Least privilege. Give the agent only the permissions it needs. An email agent that summarizes your inbox needs read access, not send access. A calendar agent that checks availability needs read access, not the ability to delete events.

Human approval for actions. Reading data is low-risk. Writing data (sending an email, booking a meeting, modifying a document) is high-risk. Design workflows so the agent proposes actions and a human approves them before execution.

Audit trails. Log every action the agent takes across every system. When the agent reads an email, searches a calendar, and drafts a response, each step should be recorded. If something goes wrong, you can trace exactly what happened.

7.9 Exercises

Exercise 1: Agent Use Case Inventory

Review your team’s recurring tasks and identify five that are multi-step, repetitive, and data-driven — good candidates for agent automation.

For each, describe the trigger (what starts the task), the steps (what happens), the output (what gets delivered), and the frequency (how often it runs). Rate each on automation potential (high, medium, or low) and risk level (high, medium, or low).

Pick the highest-potential, lowest-risk workflow as your pilot candidate and write one paragraph explaining why you would start there.

Exercise 2: Email Workflow Design

Pick one email-related task you do regularly (triaging your inbox, drafting status updates, summarizing threads for your manager, etc.).

Write a detailed prompt that describes the workflow step by step. Specify what the agent should search for, what information it should extract, what it should draft, and what requires your approval before sending.

If you have a coding agent with an email connector available, test the workflow. If not, describe the workflow precisely enough that a colleague could execute it manually.

Exercise 3: Scheduling Scenario

Imagine you need to schedule a one-hour meeting with four colleagues. Write out the constraints for each person in natural language (as they might appear in email):

  • Person A: “I’m pretty open this week, but I have a dentist appointment Wednesday morning.”
  • Person B: “Tuesday and Thursday work for me, preferably after lunch.”
  • Person C: “I’m slammed Monday and Friday. Any other day before 3 PM.”
  • Person D: “I can do Wednesday or Thursday, but not before 11.”

Work through the constraints manually and find all valid one-hour slots. Then describe how you would prompt an agent to solve this — what information would you provide, and what output would you expect?

Exercise 4: Permission Design

For each of the following agent capabilities, decide whether it should be automatic (no approval needed), require human approval, or be prohibited entirely:

  1. Reading your email inbox
  2. Sending an email on your behalf
  3. Reading your calendar
  4. Creating a calendar event
  5. Deleting a calendar event
  6. Forwarding an email to someone outside your organization
  7. Accessing a shared team calendar

For each decision, write one sentence explaining your reasoning. Compare your answers with a colleague — where do you disagree, and why?

Exercise 5: Document Processing Workflow

Give your coding agent a folder containing files in at least two different formats (for example, an Excel spreadsheet and a PDF, or a CSV and a Word document). Ask the agent to extract information from each file, combine the results, and produce a summary report.

Some ideas to get started:

  • M&A due diligence: a customer list (Excel), contract terms (PDF), screening criteria (Word), and industry benchmarks (CSV) — ask the agent to evaluate the target and produce a memo.
  • Loan risk analysis: a loan tape (Excel), banking regulations (PDF), and a risk policy (Word) — ask the agent to classify loans and flag violations.
  • Any multi-source analysis from your work: combine data from different systems or departments that arrive in different formats.

Pay attention to how the agent handles each format. Does it correctly extract the relevant information? Does it combine the sources logically? What would you need to verify before trusting the output?
